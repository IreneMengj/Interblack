Magnesia Flux

Description

Light-burned magnesia balls are made from ordinary light-burned magnesia powder which is pressed into balls after mixing. They are mainly used in converter steelmaking splashing slag to protect the furnace and can extend the furnace life.

The preparation process involves adding 80% light-burned magnesia powder to 20% water mixing and pressing for 15-20 minutes. The material is then conveyed to the ball press to form the product, which is air-dried over 48 hours until sufficient strength is achieved for sale.

Light-burned magnesia balls act as a slag component regulator in converter steelmaking. Appropriate slag viscosity and refractoriness are key, so magnesium oxide balls are added, replacing dolomite. This increases MgO content in the slag and reduces its quantity.

Due to the high melting point of magnesium oxide (2800°C), increasing its content in the slag increases viscosity and refractoriness, helping the slag bond with the furnace lining, forming a protective layer that extends furnace life and reduces consumption.

After a U.S. steel company adopted this technology, its furnace life reached nearly 20,000 heats, with a converter operating rate of 97%. This process is used in countries such as West Germany, South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan, and steel companies like Taiyuan Steel and Chengde Steel, extending furnace life to up to 12,000 heats and achieving significant economic benefits.

Requirements for Magnesia Flux

Parameter Requirement
MgO content ≥ 80%
CaO content ≤ 8%
SiO2 content ≤ 2.5%
Fe2O3 content ≤ 8%
Fractional composition ≥ 90% of the batch 10-50 mm
State Ready for use

Converter Slag Characteristics

Characteristic Value
Slag basicity (CaO + MgO) / SiO2 ≥ 2.3
Total FeO content in slag ≤ 18%
Total MgO content in slag ≥ 4%
Volume of converter slag per melt m³ 6-7
